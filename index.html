About Me

2018-current - I'm currently a Postdoctoral Researcher in Dr. Phillip Scott and Dr. Dan Beiting's lab in the Department of Pathobiology at the University of Pennsylvania (UPenn). I investigate Next-generation sequencing (NGS) datafrom experimental models and human patients with American Tegumentary Leishmaniasis. I have been using my Immunology and Cell Biology background to find expression patterns in those datasets that contribute to understanding the immune system in the infections caused by the protozoan Leishmania spp.

Additionally, I am a teaching assistant in the DIYtranscriptomics.com course at UPenn.

2017 - I was awarded a Ph.D. fellow scholarship to learn a new science field abroad. Under Dr. Dan Beiting and Dr. Phillip Scott's supervision at UPenn, I started my journey in Bioinformatics and Computational biology.

2012-2017- Throughout these years, I developed my Master's and P.hD. degrees in Immunology and Health Sciences under Dr. MD Edgar M. Carvalho's mentoring in the Hospital of Clinics and Universidade Federal da Bahia. I worked closely with human patients infected by the protozoan parasite Leishmania spp. and by the retrovirus Human T-lymphotropic virus type I (HTLV-1) to understand the clinical relevance of those infections, and also used cellular and molecular biology -wet lab- technics to characterize and understand the role of Innate and Adaptative immune cells in the defense to those pathogens.

2011 - I graduated from the Escola Bahiana de Medicina in Salvador-Bahia-Brazil with a. B.S. in Biomedical Sciences.

Recent Projects

Teaching: An Open-Source Toolkit To Expand Bioinformatics Training in Infectious Diseases

I am a Teaching Assistant in the DIYtranscriptomics.com course led by Dr. Beiting at Penn. As a short description (almost unfair), we teach from scratch how to process, analyze and interpret NGS big data without requiring apprentice's programming expertise. We use examples of real-world datasets in the context of Infectious Diseases to introduce updated Multi'Omic's concepts from the greater area of Computational Biology. Our team put together a paper gathering everything we have learned in past years.

Berry and Amorim et al. 2021, mBio, DOI: 10.1128/mBio.01214-21




The skin infection caused by Leishmania braziliensis drives an active Interferon-y systemic inflammation (start in 2019)

In this study, we were interested in evaluating whether the localized skin infection caused by the Leishmania braziliensis could impact patients' systemic immunity. Performing RNA-seq in the blood of patients from an endemic area in Brazil, we observed that the subjects exhibited this evident circulatory inflammation that was independent of the stage of the disease or size of the ulcer, for instance. We identified Interferon-gamma and Interferon-stimulated genes upregulated in the peripheral blood, mostly associated with monocytes. This data is relevant because it suggests a mechanism to limit the systemic spread of the parasite and enhance parasite control by pre-activating cells before lesion entry.

Amorim et al. 2021, PLOS NTDs, DOI: 10.1371/journal.pntd.0009321




Increased Leishmania braziliensis burden and T CD8+ cytotoxic transcriptional upregulation in the skin are biomarkers for treatment outcome in Cutaneous Leishmaniasis (start in 2017)

This was my first publication merging Immunology and Computational Biology. Here we investigated the transcriptional skin lesional profiles of patients from Brazil infected with Leishmania braziliensis, treated pentavalent antimony - the standard care for cutaneous leishmaniasis in the country. We discovered that increased parasite burden combined with an infiltration of transcriptionally active cytotoxic cells (such as CD8+ T cells and Natural killer cells) and upregulation of IL-1b in the infection site are biomarkers for delayed lesion resolution. In this study, we used R statistical programming language; dual-RNAseq mapping (quantifying both human and parasite reads from the samples); and a combination of strategies to reduce the dimensionality of the metadata to find these genetic markers. All the code involved to reproduce the analysis and images are available in a CodeOcean capsule.

Amorim et al. 2019, Science Translational Medicine, DOI: 10.1126/scitranslmed.aax4204
